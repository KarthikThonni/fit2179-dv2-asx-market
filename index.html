Behind the ASX 200

The ASX 200 is an index that tracks 200 of the largest companies listed on the Australian Securities Exchange. An index works like a market snapshot: instead of looking at one company, it combines many companies to show how a broader part of the share market is moving. This visualisation explores how the ASX 200 performed, what it is made of, and which sectors and companies had the biggest influence over the past year.

Data note: Market data was collected on 19 May 2026 at 6:15 PM. One-year returns reflect the year leading up to that collection time.

01

Global comparison

This map compares 43 countries using their main share market index or an MSCI index tracking ETF. Australia, represented by the ASX 200, ranked 28th out of 43 countries with a one-year return of 23.44%. This places Australia just below Belgium and just above Ireland.

02

About the ASX 200

Recent yearly returns

The yearly return chart gives historical context before focusing on the latest one-year period. It shows that the ASX 200 has mostly recorded positive annual returns, but the strength of those returns changes noticeably across years.

Comparing each year with the average line helps show whether a given year performed above or below the broader recent pattern.

Sector makeup

The ASX 200 is not evenly distributed across sectors. Financials and Materials make up the largest share of total market capitalisation, meaning these sectors have a major influence on how the index moves.

This means the ASX 200 is not simply a balanced collection of 200 companies. Larger sectors can shape the overall result more strongly than smaller sectors.

Company locations

ASX 200 company headquarters are concentrated in Australia’s major business centres, especially New South Wales and Victoria. This reflects the importance of Sydney and Melbourne as corporate and financial hubs.

Western Australia also has a visible presence, which is important given the role of mining and resources companies in the Australian market.

Market concentration

The concentration chart shows how quickly the largest companies add up to a major share of the ASX 200’s total market value. This helps explain why the index can be strongly affected by a relatively small number of very large companies.

Even though the ASX 200 contains 200 companies, the largest companies carry much more weight than smaller companies. This makes the index top-heavy.

03

One-year growth

Company-level growth

The treemap combines company size and one-year return. Larger rectangles represent companies with higher market capitalisation, while colour shows whether each company gained or lost value over the year.

This helps show which companies had the greatest influence on the ASX 200. A company with strong percentage growth matters more to the index when it also has a large market value.

One-year index movement

The daily area chart shows how the ASX 200 moved across the year rather than only showing the final return. The index rose overall, but the path was uneven.

The labelled troughs show two sharp falls followed by recoveries. This highlights that the final one-year result was shaped by both growth periods and short-term market shocks.

04

Sector growth analysis

Sector movement over time

This chart compares monthly sector movements as percentage change from the start of the period. The ASX 200 benchmark is included so each sector can be compared against the broader market.

Materials and Energy showed strong gains during the year, while some sectors moved below the benchmark. This helps show that the ASX 200’s overall result was shaped by very different sector paths.

Sector contribution to growth

The lollipop chart estimates each sector’s contribution to ASX 200 growth by combining sector market value with one-year return.

This is useful because a sector with moderate percentage growth can still add a large amount of value if it represents a major share of the index.

Sector return spread

The boxplot compares the spread of one-year company returns within each ASX 200 sector. This shows whether returns were consistent across a sector or driven by a wider range of company outcomes.

Some sectors contain companies with very different return patterns, while others are more tightly grouped. This helps explain why sector averages alone can hide important variation.

Company size and growth

This scatterplot compares company market capitalisation with one-year return. The logarithmic x-axis allows smaller and larger companies to be compared on the same chart.

Smaller companies show wider jumps and drops, while larger companies are more clustered. This suggests smaller companies may be more volatile, but larger companies usually have more influence on the index.
